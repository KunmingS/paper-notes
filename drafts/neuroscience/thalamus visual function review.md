## 1. Introduction

The visual thalamus has long been described as a "relay station" that passively transmits retinal signals to the cerebral cortex. This characterization, while capturing the feedforward anatomy, profoundly underestimates the computational sophistication of thalamic circuits. The lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) receives only 5-10% of its synaptic input from the retina; the remaining 90-95% originates from cortical feedback, brainstem neuromodulatory systems, the thalamic reticular nucleus (TRN), and local interneurons [1,2]. This massive convergence of non-retinal inputs endows the thalamus with the capacity to actively filter, gate, and transform visual information according to behavioral state, attentional demands, and predictive context.

Beyond the LGN, the pulvinar nucleus -- the largest thalamic nucleus in primates -- forms extensive reciprocal connections with virtually all visual cortical areas, establishing cortico-thalamo-cortical (transthalamic) loops that operate in parallel with direct corticocortical connections [3,4]. Additional visual thalamic structures, including the ventral lateral geniculate nucleus (vLGN), intergeniculate leaflet (IGL), and laterodorsal thalamic nucleus (LD), contribute to non-image-forming vision, circadian regulation, and navigation [5,6].

This review synthesizes current understanding of thalamic contributions to visual processing, organized around the central thesis that the visual thalamus is an active computational node that shapes perception, cognition, and behavior through multiple interacting mechanisms.

## 3. LGN Anatomy and Parallel Pathways

### 3.1 Primate LGN: Magnocellular, Parvocellular, and Koniocellular Streams

The primate LGN is a laminated structure comprising three major cell classes that initiate parallel processing streams. Magnocellular (M) neurons, located in the ventral two layers, have large receptive fields, high contrast sensitivity, transient responses, and project primarily to cortical layer 4Ca. Parvocellular (P) neurons, occupying the dorsal four layers, exhibit smaller receptive fields, sustained responses, color opponency, and project to layer 4Cb. Koniocellular (K) neurons, intercalated between the principal layers, are heterogeneous in properties and project to superficial cortical layers [7,8]. Recent single-cell transcriptomic analysis of macaque LGN has revealed novel molecular subpopulations within these classical divisions, suggesting greater cell-type diversity than the canonical M/P/K trichotomy [9].

### 3.2 Cat LGN: X, Y, and W Pathways

In the cat, LGN neurons are classified as X (linear spatial summation, sustained responses, small receptive fields), Y (nonlinear summation, transient responses, large fields), and W (heterogeneous, sluggish) types. These parallel pathways prefigured the primate M/P/K classification and established the principle that distinct functional streams are maintained from retina through thalamus to cortex [7].

### 3.3 Mouse LGN: Emerging Diversity

The mouse dLGN, initially considered a simpler, unlaminated structure, has proven to harbor significant functional and molecular diversity. Bienkowski et al. (2019) demonstrated that the mouse dLGN projects not only to primary visual cortex (V1) but also to multiple extrastriate areas, challenging the view that all visual information must traverse V1 before reaching higher cortical areas [10]. A high-resolution transcriptomic atlas of the ventral visual thalamus has further revealed distinct cell types in the dLGN, vLGN, and IGL [11]. These findings establish the mouse as a valuable model for dissecting thalamic circuit mechanisms with modern genetic tools.

## 4. LGN Interneurons and Local Inhibitory Circuits

### 4.1 Presynaptic Dendrites and F2 Terminals

LGN interneurons are unique in possessing bifunctional neurites that serve both dendritic (input-receiving) and axonal (output-releasing) functions. Their dendrites release GABA from specialized F2 terminals that form part of triadic synaptic arrangements (glomeruli), where a retinal terminal contacts both a relay cell dendrite and an interneuron dendrite, and the interneuron dendrite in turn inhibits the relay cell [12]. Acuna-Goycolea et al. (2008) demonstrated that active dendritic conductances -- including T-type calcium channels and sodium channels -- dynamically regulate GABA release from interneuron dendrites, providing a local gain control mechanism at the first synapse of the visual pathway [13].

### 4.2 Functional Specialization of Individual Interneurons

Morgan and Lichtman (2020) used serial-section electron microscopy to map the complete input-output connectivity of a single mouse dLGN interneuron, revealing that it innervated 256 thalamocortical cells spread across functionally distinct subregions [14]. Despite this broad output, the neuron's neurites were almost universally bifunctional, receiving and transmitting at the same processes. This suggests that individual interneurons participate in many forms of inhibition simultaneously.

Mullner and Roska (2024) showed that mouse dLGN interneurons exhibit a surprising degree of retinal input specialization, receiving inputs from specific retinal ganglion cell types rather than integrating indiscriminately across channels [15]. This functional selectivity positions interneurons as channel-specific modulators rather than global gain controllers.

### 4.3 Feedforward Inhibition

Blitz and Regehr (2005) identified two temporally distinct types of feedforward inhibition in the LGN: a fast component with identical latency to excitation (mediated by F2 dendritic terminals) and a slower component mediated by conventional axonal outputs [12]. The fast component is ideally suited to sharpen the temporal precision of retinal signals, while the slower component may regulate gain over longer timescales.

## 5. Retinogeniculate Transmission: Temporal Filtering and Gain Control

### 5.1 The Retinogeniculate Synapse as a Dynamic Filter

Although retinal inputs constitute a small minority of synapses onto LGN relay cells, they are the primary drivers of receptive field properties [1,2]. The retinogeniculate synapse is not a simple point-to-point relay but a dynamic filter whose transfer characteristics depend on stimulus conditions and behavioral state. Usrey and colleagues have systematically characterized how this synapse transforms the retinal signal. Alitto et al. (2019) demonstrated that contrast gain control operates at the retinogeniculate synapse itself, with the efficacy and timing of retinal spike transmission varying as a function of stimulus contrast [16]. Alexander et al. (2022) further showed that temporal integration windows in the LGN are dynamic, shifting between short and long integration modes depending on the temporal structure of retinal input [17].

### 5.2 Extraclassical Surround Suppression

Fisher et al. (2017) dissected the relative contributions of retinal and non-retinal circuits to extraclassical surround suppression in the LGN [18]. While retinal inputs provide a component of suppression, non-retinal sources -- including cortical feedback and TRN inhibition -- contribute substantially, particularly for large stimuli. This represents a clear case where the LGN actively reshapes the visual signal beyond what it inherits from the retina.

### 5.3 Synergistic Geniculate and Cortical Processing

Elsayed et al. (2026) demonstrated that decorrelation of spatial frequency representations across the visual hierarchy involves synergistic dynamics between LGN and V1, with coarse-to-fine temporal processing emerging partly from geniculate filtering rather than being solely a cortical computation [19].

## 6. Corticothalamic Feedback: Layer 6 and Stream-Specific Modulation

### 6.1 Anatomy and Physiology of the Corticogeniculate Pathway

Corticothalamic feedback from V1 layer 6 to the LGN is massive, outnumbering retinal inputs by approximately 10:1 in terms of synapses [1]. These feedback projections are organized in parallel streams that match the feedforward M, P, and K pathways. Briggs and Usrey (2009) demonstrated that distinct morphological classes of corticogeniculate neurons in macaque V1 target specific LGN layers, establishing stream-specific feedback channels [20]. This parallel organization extends beyond V1: Briggs et al. (2016) showed that V2 also sends direct corticogeniculate feedback with morphological substrates for parallel stream targeting [21], and Adusei et al. (2024) demonstrated that mid-level extrastriate areas (V3, V4) contribute additional parallel feedback channels to the LGN [22].

### 6.2 Functional Effects of Corticogeniculate Feedback

The functional impact of corticogeniculate feedback has been debated, in part because experimental approaches differ in their ability to isolate this pathway. Andolina et al. (2007) showed that corticothalamic feedback enhances the temporal precision of LGN responses to drifting gratings, sharpening stimulus-locked timing without necessarily changing mean firing rates [23]. Wang et al. (2018) demonstrated that focal enhancement of layer 6 activity produces gain control of thalamic receptive fields, with effects that depend on the spatial relationship between cortical and thalamic receptive field positions [24].

Mai et al. (2025) used optogenetics to selectively manipulate corticogeniculate feedback in both monkeys and ferrets, revealing dual effects: stream-specific improvements in spatial resolution (for P-stream targets) alongside generalized gain modulation across all streams [25]. Hasse and Briggs (2017) similarly found that optogenetic suppression of corticogeniculate feedback reduced both spatial resolution and temporal precision in the ferret LGN [26]. These convergent findings establish that feedback sharpens and refines the very signals it receives back.

### 6.3 Feedback from Higher Cortical Areas

Jones et al. (2013) provided evidence that feedback from the motion-processing area MT, cascading through V1 layer 6, influences LGN responses to moving stimuli [27]. This demonstrates that the LGN is subject to feedback modulation from areas far beyond primary visual cortex, integrating high-level motion signals into the earliest stages of central visual processing.

## 7. Thalamic Reticular Nucleus and Attentional Gating

### 7.1 Crick's Searchlight Hypothesis and Its Validation

Francis Crick (1984) proposed that the TRN acts as an attentional "searchlight," selectively gating information flow from thalamus to cortex [28]. This hypothesis received direct support from McAlonan et al. (2006, 2008), who recorded from TRN neurons in macaques performing attentional tasks [29,30]. They found that TRN neurons showed increased activity when attention was directed away from their receptive fields, consistent with a suppressive gating function. Crucially, these attentional modulations in TRN preceded those in the LGN, supporting a causal role for TRN in gating visual transmission. Alitto et al. (2025) further showed that spatial attention produces weak but significant modulation of LGN visual responses in macaques, consistent with a subtle thalamic gating mechanism [31].

### 7.2 State-Dependent TRN Architecture

Halassa et al. (2014) combined the first TRN ensemble recordings with optogenetic tagging to reveal that the TRN contains functionally distinct subnetworks whose architecture changes with behavioral state [32]. During attention, sensory-sector TRN neurons became more precisely tuned and more decorrelated, supporting selective gating. During sleep, the same neurons shifted to more synchronized, broadly tuned activity patterns that promote cortical spindles.

Wimmer et al. (2015) demonstrated a causal role for TRN in sensory selection during divided attention, showing that prefrontal cortex regulates thalamic sensory selection through TRN circuits [33]. This established a prefrontal-TRN-thalamus pathway as a key mechanism for top-down attentional control.

### 7.3 Molecular Diversity and Circuit Specificity

Li et al. (2020) identified molecularly distinct TRN subnetworks defined by calbindin (CB) and somatostatin (SOM) expression, with CB neurons targeting first-order sensory thalamic nuclei and SOM neurons targeting higher-order nuclei [34]. Vaughn et al. (2023) showed that these subtypes are linked by heterocellular electrical synapses, enabling coordinated inhibition across functionally distinct thalamic circuits [35]. Ahrens et al. (2015) demonstrated that ErbB4 signaling in SOM-expressing TRN neurons regulates sensory selection behavior, linking a specific molecular mechanism to attentional gating function [36].

### 7.4 Prefrontal Control of TRN

Zikopoulos and Barbas (2006) showed that prefrontal projections to TRN in primates have a distinct mode of termination compared to sensory cortical projections: prefrontal axons form large boutons in the anterior TRN connected to limbic and association thalamic nuclei, providing a structural basis for executive control of thalamic gating [37].

## 8. Burst versus Tonic Firing: State-Dependent Processing

### 8.1 T-Type Calcium Channels and Firing Modes

Thalamic relay neurons exhibit two distinct firing modes governed by T-type calcium channels (CaV3.x). In the tonic mode, when the membrane is depolarized, neurons fire single spikes that faithfully relay retinal input. In the burst mode, when the membrane is hyperpolarized (e.g., during drowsiness or inattention), deinactivation of T-type channels enables low-threshold calcium spikes crowned by high-frequency bursts of action potentials [1,38].

### 8.2 Functional Significance of Burst and Tonic Modes

Sherman (2001) proposed that burst firing serves as a "wake-up call" that detects novel or salient stimuli during states of inattention, while tonic firing provides a linear relay mode for detailed stimulus analysis during attentive states [39]. Weyand et al. (2001) provided physiological support by recording LGN neurons across sleep-wake transitions, demonstrating that bursts are prevalent during slow-wave sleep but also occur during wakefulness, particularly in response to novel stimuli [40].

### 8.3 Higher-Order Relays Burst More

Ramcharan et al. (2005) compared burst and tonic firing across first-order and higher-order thalamic nuclei in awake primates, finding that higher-order relays (including the pulvinar) exhibit substantially more bursting than first-order relays like the LGN [41]. This suggests that higher-order nuclei may operate in a more nonlinear, state-dependent regime, consistent with their proposed role in attention and cognitive processing.

### 8.4 Predictive Homeostasis

Hong et al. (2014) proposed a computational framework in which T-type calcium channels in LGN neurons implement "predictive homeostasis" -- maintaining optimal input-output sensitivity by adjusting membrane excitability in response to the recent history of synaptic drive [42]. This reframes burst/tonic transitions not merely as state indicators but as an active mechanism for sensory prediction.

## 9. Neuromodulatory Control of the Visual Thalamus

### 9.1 Cholinergic Modulation

Acetylcholine (ACh) from brainstem (pedunculopontine and laterodorsal tegmental nuclei) and basal forebrain nuclei profoundly influences thalamic processing. ACh depolarizes relay cells (promoting tonic firing) while hyperpolarizing TRN neurons (reducing inhibition), together enhancing signal transmission during arousal and attention [1,43]. Sokhadze et al. (2022) provided a comprehensive map of cholinergic projections to the mouse visual thalamus, revealing topographically organized inputs from distinct brainstem sources to dLGN, vLGN, and TRN [44]. The postnatal development of cholinergic input to TRN follows a protracted timeline (Sokhadze et al., 2019), suggesting that maturation of attentional gating depends on the gradual establishment of these modulatory projections [45].

### 9.2 Noradrenergic Modulation

Norepinephrine from the locus coeruleus (LC) similarly promotes tonic firing in relay cells and modulates TRN activity, but with distinct receptor pharmacology and spatial targeting compared to ACh. Bickford et al. (2000) characterized the neurotransmitter content of subcortical extraretinal inputs to the primate LGN, demonstrating the diversity of modulatory systems converging on geniculate relay cells [46].

### 9.3 Histaminergic Modulation

Tripodi and Asari (2025) identified a previously underappreciated role for histamine in visual thalamic processing: activation of the central histaminergic system slowed visual responses in both retina and LGN of awake mice, suggesting that histamine acts as a global modulator of temporal processing speed across early visual stages [47].

### 9.4 Presynaptic Modulation

Chen and Regehr (2003) demonstrated that neuromodulators including serotonin and GABA exert presynaptic effects at the retinogeniculate synapse itself, modifying release probability and short-term plasticity independently of postsynaptic effects on relay cell excitability [48]. This adds a presynaptic dimension to the modulation of visual signal transmission.

## 10. Thalamocortical Oscillations: Alpha and Gamma Rhythms

### 10.1 Alpha Rhythms and Thalamic Pacing

The posterior alpha rhythm (8-13 Hz) has long been associated with visual cortical idling, but accumulating evidence points to a thalamic origin and active functional role. Lorincz et al. (2009) showed that LGN relay cells in behaving cats form two functionally distinct groups whose cumulative firing undergoes cyclic suppression locked to the alpha rhythm, with phasic inhibition from local interneurons providing the temporal frame [49]. This demonstrates that alpha oscillations actively gate the relay of visual information through pulsatile inhibition.

### 10.2 Sleep Spindles and Slow Oscillations

Steriade and colleagues established that the TRN is the pacemaker of sleep spindles (7-15 Hz), a hallmark rhythm of early NREM sleep [50,51]. Halassa et al. (2011) provided causal evidence using optogenetics: selective drive of TRN neurons switched thalamocortical firing from tonic to burst mode and generated state-dependent cortical spindles [52]. Steriade (1997) further demonstrated that the cerebral cortex and thalamus constitute a unified oscillatory machine, with corticothalamic volleys synchronizing both fast (20-50 Hz) and slow (<15 Hz) oscillations [53].

### 10.3 Gamma Oscillations and Perceptual Encoding

Perrenoud et al. (2025) developed a novel analytical approach revealing that gamma activity (30-80 Hz) in mouse V1 consists of discrete network events rather than sustained oscillations, with these gamma events flexibly encoding perceptual information and being modulated by thalamocortical inputs [54]. This reframes thalamocortical gamma interactions as event-driven rather than rhythmic processes.

### 10.4 Cholinergic Mediation of Oscillatory Attention

Sajedin et al. (2025) showed that cortical cholinergic inputs mediate attentional modulation by enhancing coherence between cortical layers in macaque V1 and V4, with effects on both gamma and alpha bands [55]. This links neuromodulatory control of the thalamocortical system to the oscillatory mechanisms of attention.

## 11. Pulvinar: Anatomy, Connectivity, and Transthalamic Circuits

### 11.1 The Largest Thalamic Nucleus

The pulvinar is the largest nucleus in the primate thalamus and the dominant extrageniculate visual nucleus in all mammals (called the lateral posterior nucleus, LP, in non-primates). It is subdivided into inferior, lateral, medial, and anterior divisions, each with distinct connectivity patterns [3,56]. Cortes et al. (2024) described the pulvinar as a hub for visual processing and cortical integration, emphasizing its reciprocal connections with virtually all visual cortical areas [57].

### 11.2 Transthalamic Pathways

Layer 5 cortical neurons provide driving input to the pulvinar, forming the basis of cortico-thalamo-cortical (transthalamic) circuits that provide an alternative route for communication between cortical areas [1,4]. McKinnon et al. (2025) provided the first causal evidence that disruption of transthalamic circuits from V1 through the pulvinar impairs visual discrimination in mice, establishing a behavioral requirement for this pathway [58].

Cassidy et al. (2025) demonstrated that mouse driver and modulator cortico-thalamo-cortical circuits through the pulvinar have complementary organization: driver pathways (from L5) link specific cortical areas in a hierarchical pattern, while modulator pathways (from L6) show more diffuse connectivity [59]. de Souza et al. (2021) showed that the laminar patterns of cortical projections to the pulvinar in cats and mice follow the same rules that define feedforward and feedback corticocortical connections, suggesting that transthalamic routes respect the cortical hierarchy [60].

### 11.3 Pulvinar Modulation of Cortical Responses

de Souza et al. (2020) demonstrated that pulvinar inactivation altered contrast response functions differently in primary versus higher-order visual cortex in the cat, with stronger effects in higher cortical areas [61]. This gradient of influence supports the concept that the pulvinar's functional role increases at higher levels of the cortical hierarchy. Vanni et al. (2015) used voltage-sensitive dye imaging to show that electrical stimulation of the dLGN and pulvinar evoke spatiotemporally distinct cortical activation patterns, consistent with their different projection targets (layer 4 for dLGN, layer 1 for pulvinar in V1) [62].

## 12. Pulvinar and Attention: Coordinating Cortical Communication

### 12.1 Alpha-Band Synchronization and Attentional Routing

Saalmann et al. (2012) provided a landmark demonstration that the pulvinar regulates information transmission between cortical areas based on attention demands [63]. During a visual attention task, the pulvinar synchronized alpha-band (8-15 Hz) oscillations in V4 and temporal-occipital cortex, with the degree of synchronization predicting behavioral performance. This alpha-frequency coupling was directed from pulvinar to cortex, establishing the pulvinar as an active coordinator of cortical communication rather than a passive relay.

### 12.2 Cognitive and Perceptual Functions

Saalmann and Kastner (2011) synthesized evidence for cognitive and perceptual functions of the visual thalamus, proposing that both the LGN and pulvinar implement gain control mechanisms that regulate information flow according to attention and behavioral context [64]. The pulvinar, through its widespread cortical connections, is positioned to modulate processing at multiple levels of the visual hierarchy simultaneously.

### 12.3 Causal Role in Cortico-Cortical Interactions

Eradath et al. (2021) provided evidence for a causal role of the pulvinar in coordinating task-independent cortico-cortical interactions, demonstrating that pulvinar perturbation disrupted functional connectivity between cortical areas even in the absence of specific attentional demands [65]. Bourgeois et al. (2019) further argued for the pulvinar as an integrative controller of attention through its interactions with both dorsal and ventral attention networks [66].

### 12.4 The Pulvinar's Role in Cognition

Cortes et al. (2026) synthesized evidence that pulvinar circuits shape perception and cognition through three computational operations: precision-weighted gain for routing information across visual hierarchies, priority mapping for stimulus selection, and temporal alignment for feature binding and multisensory integration [67]. Froesel et al. (2021) broadened the perspective by reviewing the pulvinar's contribution to multisensory integration, emphasizing its role in fast subcortical detection and selection beyond the purely visual domain [68].

## 13. The Driver-Modulator Framework: First-Order and Higher-Order Relays

### 13.1 Drivers versus Modulators

Sherman and Guillery (1998, 2002) proposed a fundamental distinction between driver and modulator inputs to thalamic relay cells [1,2,69]. Drivers determine the basic receptive field properties of their target neurons, are characterized by large (RL-type) synaptic terminals, ionotropic glutamate receptor activation, paired-pulse depression, and all-or-none EPSPs. Modulators, in contrast, exert subtle, gain-controlling influences through smaller (RS-type) terminals, metabotropic receptor activation, and paired-pulse facilitation. In the LGN, retinal inputs are drivers while cortical feedback and brainstem inputs are modulators.

### 13.2 First-Order versus Higher-Order Nuclei

This framework extends to a classification of thalamic nuclei: first-order nuclei (e.g., LGN) receive their driver input from subcortical sources and relay this information to primary cortical areas; higher-order nuclei (e.g., pulvinar) receive their driver input from cortical layer 5 and relay information between cortical areas [1,2]. Van Horn and Sherman (2007) provided ultrastructural support by demonstrating that higher-order nuclei have proportionally fewer driver (RL) synapses than first-order nuclei [70]. Wang et al. (2002) quantified the relative distribution of synaptic types in the cat pulvinar, confirming the expected profile for a higher-order nucleus [71].

### 13.3 Functional Validation

Kirchgessner et al. (2021) used optogenetics to functionally validate the driver-modulator distinction in corticothalamic pathways to the pulvinar, demonstrating that layer 5 inputs produce large, depressing EPSPs (driver signature) while layer 6 inputs produce smaller, facilitating EPSPs (modulator signature) [72]. Miller-Hansen and Sherman (2022) showed that conserved patterns of functional organization apply across sensory modalities, with first-order and higher-order thalamic circuits maintaining parallel driver and modulator channels in both somatosensory and visual systems [73].

### 13.4 Transthalamic Implications

Sherman (2005) articulated the implication that every cortical area has both a direct corticocortical output and a parallel transthalamic route through higher-order thalamus, fundamentally changing the conceptualization of cortical hierarchies [74]. Guillery and Sherman (2002) generalized from the visual system to propose that thalamic relays are not merely sensory waypoints but active participants in corticocortical communication [69]. Wurtz et al. (2005) extended the framework by showing that the superior colliculus provides driver input to higher-order thalamic nuclei, establishing a subcortical driver source parallel to the cortical L5 pathway [75].

## 14. Predictive Processing in the Visual Thalamus

### 14.1 Thalamocortical Prediction Error Circuits

Furutachi et al. (2024) demonstrated a cooperative thalamocortical circuit mechanism for sensory prediction errors in mouse visual cortex [76]. Higher-order thalamic (LP/pulvinar) neurons convey prediction-error signals to V1, where they interact with local inhibitory interneurons to augment cortical responses to unexpected visual stimuli. This finding places the thalamus at the center of predictive processing, challenging purely cortical models of prediction error computation.

Ross and Hamm (2024) contextualized this finding within an expanding repertoire of circuit mechanisms for visual prediction errors, noting that thalamic contributions to predictive processing represent a paradigm shift in understanding sensory cortical computation [77].

### 14.2 T-Type Channels as Predictive Homeostats

Hong et al. (2014) proposed that T-type calcium channels in LGN neurons implement predictive homeostasis at the cellular level, maintaining optimal sensitivity by adjusting excitability based on recent input history [42]. Kim et al. (2015) tested this framework across sensory and motor thalamic nuclei, finding that T-type channel activation patterns differ between sensory nuclei (where they counteract predictable input patterns) and motor nuclei (where they generate output patterns) [78].

### 14.3 Implications for Hierarchical Predictive Coding

The placement of prediction-error computation in thalamocortical rather than purely cortical circuits has profound implications for hierarchical predictive coding theories. If higher-order thalamic nuclei carry prediction errors from higher to lower cortical areas via transthalamic routes, then the thalamus serves not merely as a relay but as a critical computational node in the brain's generative model of the world.

## 15. Non-Classical Visual Thalamic Nuclei: vLGN, IGL, and Laterodorsal Thalamus

### 15.1 Ventral LGN and Intergeniculate Leaflet

The vLGN and IGL are retinorecipient structures associated primarily with non-image-forming visual functions including circadian photoentrainment, visuomotor gating, and mood regulation. Unlike the dLGN, which is dominated by excitatory relay neurons, the vLGN contains predominantly GABAergic neurons organized into distinct subtype-specific sublaminae [79]. Sabbagh et al. (2021) demonstrated that diverse GABAergic neuron types in the vLGN are organized into molecularly and spatially distinct sublaminae, suggesting circuit-level specialization for different non-image-forming functions [79].

Stebbins et al. (2026) generated a high-resolution transcriptomic atlas of the ventral visual thalamus, revealing cell-type diversity in vLGN and IGL that rivals the complexity of the dLGN [11]. Stebbins et al. (2024) further showed that retinal input is required for the maintenance of neuronal laminae in the vLGN, demonstrating that activity-dependent mechanisms shape the organization of non-image-forming visual circuits [80].

### 15.2 Multisensory Integration in vLGN

Huang et al. (2026) redefined the vLGN/IGL as a multimodal hub orchestrating stress resilience, demonstrating that vLGN/IGL neurons integrate visual and non-visual sensory inputs and mediate adaptive stress responses through locus coeruleus-related circuits [5]. This transforms the understanding of the vLGN from a purely visual structure to a multisensory integration center linking environmental cues to behavioral state.

### 15.3 Non-Image-Forming Visual Circuits

Hu et al. (2024) identified a visual circuit involving the vLGN and the parabrachial nucleus that mediates the antipruritic effects of bright light treatment, demonstrating a previously unknown link between non-image-forming vision and somatosensory modulation [81]. Shang et al. (2024) showed that fear of heights in mice depends on non-image-forming visual processing rather than classical image-forming pathways, highlighting the behavioral significance of these subcortical visual circuits [82]. Feord et al. (2023) demonstrated widespread colour opponency across the mouse subcortical visual system, including vLGN and other retinorecipient nuclei [83].

### 15.4 Laterodorsal Thalamic Nucleus and Navigation

Li et al. (2025) discovered that the laterodorsal thalamic nucleus (LD), positioned at the intersection of visual and navigation circuits, encodes optic flow direction and turning during spatial navigation in mice [6]. LD neurons exhibit strong direction selectivity with a dominant preference for contralateral-to-ipsilateral visual motion, mirroring horizontal optic flow during ipsilateral turning. This establishes the LD as a bridge between visual input and navigational computation.

Perry and Mitchell (2019) reviewed the LD as a higher-order thalamic relay based on its receipt of driver input from cortical layer 5 of the retrosplenial and visual association cortices [84]. Shinkai et al. (2005) demonstrated dual axonal terminations from retrosplenial and visual cortices in the LD, with morphological characteristics consistent with the driver-modulator framework [85].

## 16. Development, Plasticity, and Critical Periods

### 16.1 Retinogeniculate Refinement

The development of mature retinogeniculate connectivity involves a protracted period of synaptic refinement during which an initial overabundance of weak retinal inputs is pruned to a small number of strong inputs. Lin et al. (2014) showed that changes in input strength and number are driven by distinct mechanisms at the retinogeniculate synapse, with strengthening depending on NMDA receptor activation and elimination requiring competition between converging inputs [86]. Zhang et al. (2025) demonstrated eye-specific differences in active zone addition during synaptic competition, revealing that winning inputs from the dominant eye add release sites more rapidly during the critical period [87].

### 16.2 Molecular Signals in Retinogeniculate Targeting

Su et al. (2011) discovered that the extracellular matrix protein Reelin is required for class-specific retinogeniculate targeting, with Reelin mutant mice showing disrupted segregation of image-forming (to dLGN) and non-image-forming (to vLGN/IGL) retinal ganglion cell axons [88]. Golding et al. (2014) demonstrated that retinal input directs the recruitment of inhibitory interneurons into thalamic visual circuits, showing that circuit activity is required for migration, molecular differentiation, and functional integration of dLGN interneurons [89]. Menna et al. (2003) showed that anterogradely transported BDNF from retinal ganglion cells promotes retinal axon remodeling during eye-specific segregation [90].

### 16.3 Reassessing LGN Plasticity

Stacy and Van Hooser (2022) reviewed new evidence that the LGN undergoes more experience-dependent plasticity than traditionally assumed [91]. While early studies emphasized cortical plasticity, recent work has revealed that altered visual experience affects functional properties of LGN neurons, including spatial frequency tuning and temporal response properties, particularly during a critical period that overlaps with cortical plasticity windows.

## 17. The Thalamus as an Integrative Hub

### 17.1 From Relay to Hub

The accumulated evidence reviewed above transforms the conceptualization of the visual thalamus from a simple relay to an integrative hub that actively shapes visual processing at multiple levels. Saalmann and Kastner (2015) articulated the concept of the "cognitive thalamus," arguing that both first-order and higher-order thalamic nuclei actively regulate information flow to cortex based on behavioral demands [92].

### 17.2 Subcortical Network Hub

Cambareri et al. (2025) used 7T resting-state fMRI to map subcortical hubs sustaining human consciousness, identifying the pulvinar and other thalamic nuclei as critical connector nodes linking canonical cortical networks [93]. Yu and He (2025) demonstrated that transcranial focused ultrasound targeting the visual thalamus modulates activity in interconnected visual cortical areas in non-human primates, providing direct evidence for the thalamus's hub role in distributing and modulating cortical visual processing [94].

### 17.3 Multiple Roles of the Visual Thalamus

The visual thalamus performs at least five computationally distinct operations: (1) temporal filtering and gain control of retinal signals (LGN); (2) state-dependent gating via burst/tonic mode switching (LGN, TRN); (3) attentional selection through TRN-mediated inhibition; (4) coordination of cortical communication through pulvinar-mediated synchronization; and (5) predictive processing via higher-order thalamic prediction error signals. These operations are not independent but interact through shared neuromodulatory control and recurrent thalamocortical loops.

## 18. Discussion and Future Directions

### 18.1 Key Themes

Three themes emerge from this review. First, the visual thalamus operates through parallel, stream-specific channels at every level -- from retinogeniculate to corticogeniculate to transthalamic pathways. Second, the thalamus is a critical substrate for state-dependent processing, implementing the transitions between sleep, drowsiness, passive viewing, and active attention that profoundly shape visual perception. Third, the thalamus is embedded in recurrent loops at multiple scales, from local interneuron triads to global cortico-thalamo-cortical circuits, and these loops implement computations (gain control, prediction error, temporal coordination) that cannot be achieved by feedforward processing alone.

### 18.2 Open Questions

Several fundamental questions remain unresolved:

**Cell-type specificity in the LGN.** Single-cell transcriptomics is revealing molecular diversity beyond the classical M/P/K or X/Y/W classifications [9,11]. How does this molecular diversity map onto functional heterogeneity, and what circuit roles do newly identified cell types play?

**The computational role of corticogeniculate feedback.** Despite decades of study, the precise computation implemented by layer 6 feedback remains debated. Does it primarily sharpen spatial resolution, implement predictive coding, or provide contextual modulation? The development of cell-type-specific optogenetic tools in primates may finally resolve this question.

**Pulvinar contributions to conscious perception.** The pulvinar's role in coordinating cortical processing and its disruption in disorders of consciousness suggest a fundamental contribution to visual awareness, but causal evidence in humans remains limited.

**Predictive processing mechanisms.** Furutachi et al. (2024) opened a new direction by demonstrating thalamocortical prediction error circuits [76], but the full architecture of predictive processing in the thalamus -- including the roles of different cell types, neuromodulators, and oscillatory mechanisms -- remains to be elucidated.

**Non-image-forming thalamic circuits.** The recent discoveries of multisensory integration in vLGN [5] and optic flow encoding in LD [6] suggest that our understanding of the "visual" thalamus is incomplete. These non-classical circuits may play important roles in navigation, arousal, and emotional regulation.

### 18.3 Methodological Advances

Several technical advances are poised to accelerate progress. Large-scale Neuropixels recordings enable simultaneous monitoring of thalamic and cortical populations across behavioral states. Cell-type-specific optogenetics and chemogenetics allow precise manipulation of defined thalamic circuits. Transcranial focused ultrasound provides non-invasive modulation of thalamic nuclei in primates and humans [94]. Single-cell multi-omics are revealing the molecular logic of thalamic cell-type diversity. Together, these tools promise to move the field from correlation to mechanism, testing causal hypotheses about thalamic computation in behaving animals.

### 18.4 Clinical Relevance

Thalamic dysfunction has been implicated in a range of neurological and psychiatric conditions including schizophrenia, autism spectrum disorder, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, epilepsy, and disorders of consciousness [32,34,93]. Understanding the precise circuit mechanisms by which the thalamus shapes visual and cognitive processing will be essential for developing targeted therapeutic interventions.
